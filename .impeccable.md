## Design Context

### Users
**Primary:** Mixed audience — business owners who read their P&L once a month, and CFOs/finance directors who live inside spreadsheets. The interface serves both; it adapts depth on demand rather than picking one audience.

**Context of use:** Late nights in an office or at a desk, staring at documents that contain uncomfortable truths. People who need answers fast, who distrust dashboards that look prettier than they are useful. MENA-region finance professionals (Arabic + English).

**Job to be done:** Understand what the numbers are actually saying, find the risk before it becomes a crisis, get a trusted second opinion without scheduling a meeting.

### Brand Personality
**Three words:** Dense. Precise. Adaptive.

Not "modern." Not "elegant." Dense like a well-organized trading terminal. Precise like a signed audit report. Adaptive like a senior CFO who can talk to a founder and a board in the same afternoon.

**Emotional goals:** The user should feel like they have a trusted advisor in the room — not a bot, not a dashboard, not a toy. Confidence from clarity. The relief of someone who finally understands what their numbers mean.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Theme:** Dark — always. Finance professionals work at night, stress-test models at 11pm, and need a screen that doesn't fight them. Light mode would feel like a consumer product.

**Palette:** Deep navy backgrounds (existing: #05060f → #0d0e1f range), amber accent (existing: #f59e0b). This is the one warm signal in a cold, precise interface. Do not add more accent colors. Resist the urge to introduce green-only for "positive" — amber is the brand.

**Anti-references:**
- Generic SaaS dashboard — no cookie-cutter card grids, no teal/purple gradients, no stock icon packs, no hero metric templates (big number + small label + gradient blob)
- Bloomberg terminal — dense but not hostile; information without walls of text
- Consumer finance apps (Mint, Copilot) — too playful, oversimplified, feels like a toy

**References (feel, not copy):**
- A Bloomberg terminal but with a UX designer in the room
- The interior of a Reuters trading desk: purposeful density, nothing decorative
- A printed financial audit report: monospace numbers, clean hierarchy, no frills

### Design Principles
1. **Information density is a feature.** Pack in data, but control hierarchy ruthlessly. Not everything needs a card. Not everything needs space.
2. **Every element earns its place.** No decorative elements. No glassmorphism. No border-left accent stripes. If it doesn't carry meaning, remove it.
3. **Amber is the one point of warmth.** Do not dilute with more accent colors. The single amber signal against cold navy is what makes CFO Pulse feel intentional, not templated.
4. **Progressive depth.** Surface for owners, drill-down for CFOs. The same interface serves both by revealing complexity on demand.
5. **Monospace for numbers, always.** Financial figures in monospace ensure column alignment and signal precision. Mixed fonts in tables are a design crime.
6. **Honest hierarchy.** If the margin is thin, the text color should reflect that — not be softened with "neutral" grays to avoid alarming the user.
